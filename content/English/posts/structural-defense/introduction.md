Products liability law has, since its modern inception, relied on a behavioral inference chain. In *Greenman v. Yuba Power Products, Inc.*, the California Supreme Court held that a manufacturer is strictly liable when a product "proves to have a defect that causes injury," and that the plaintiff need not prove negligence—the defect can be inferred from the product's failure during normal operation. (59 Cal. 2d 57, 62–64 (1963).) The Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability codified this inference chain: §3 provides that a plaintiff may prove a defect through circumstantial evidence of malfunction alone, without identifying the specific defect; §2 then classifies the defect as manufacturing (§2(a)), design (§2(b)), or warning (§2(c)), determining the applicable liability standard. (Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability §§ 2–3 (1998).)

This inference chain works for physical products because the relationship between observable failure and architectural location is relatively constrained. When a tire blows out, the forensic evidence—tread separation pattern, manufacturing batch records, design specifications—can distinguish between a manufacturing defect (this tire), a design defect (this model), and a warning defect (inadequate inflation instructions). The behavioral evidence constrains the architectural hypothesis space sufficiently for §2 classification to proceed.

AI systems break this chain. The mathematical result is now established: *Dual Certificates* proves that for any agent whose operative state exceeds its visible trace, output-only observation cannot recover the internal state distribution. The static certificate $_state^UB$ upper-bounds residual hidden-state entropy; the dynamic certificate $_act^LB$ lower-bounds residual decision relevance via probe variables. The pair proves that structural unrecoverability and behavioral activation are independent axes: a system can have large hidden capacity that is dormant (structurally unrecoverable but behaviorally inert) or active (structurally unrecoverable and behaviorally consequential). Neither axis is visible to output-only review.

For tort law, the implication is specific and destabilizing. The §3 Malfunction Doctrine correctly resolves that behavioral failure—the AI produced harmful output—proves *a* defect exists. But it cannot resolve *which* §2 category the defect belongs to, because identical behavioral evidence (the same harmful output pattern) can be produced by a manufacturing defect (corrupted weights in this specific deployment instance), a design defect (systemic sycophancy in the base model), or a warning defect (inadequate interface disclosures about session-length risk). The behavioral evidence underdetermines the architectural location of the defect, and §2 classification presupposes that location is known.

This is not a failure of evidence gathering. It is a structural property of the technology: the information needed to distinguish between L1 (Environment), L2 (Interface), L3 (Algorithm & Weights), and L4 (Strategic Authority) failures is partitioned from the output observer by the epistemic boundary that *Dual Certificates* formalizes. More discovery, better experts, or finer-grained output analysis cannot cross this boundary; the boundary is architectural, not evidentiary.

The practical consequence is judicial paralysis. Courts confronted with AI harm can determine that a defect exists (§3) but cannot classify it (§2), and therefore cannot determine the applicable liability standard. This paralysis is not speculative. In *Garcia v. Character Technologies*, the court held that AI chatbot outputs constitute a "product" subject to strict products liability—resolving the threshold question of whether products liability applies at all—but the §2 classification question (was the defect in design, manufacturing, or warning?) remains unresolved. (*Garcia v. Character Techs., Inc.*, No. 6:24-cv-01903-ACC-UAM, Order on Motion to Dismiss at 28–34 (M.D. Fla. May 21, 2025) (Conway, J.).) In *Soelberg v. OpenAI*, the complaint alleges harms produced by a known sycophancy defect, but the pleadings oscillate between design defect, failure-to-warn, and negligence theories without a structural framework for choosing among them. (First County Bank v. OpenAI Found., No. CGC-25-631477 (Cal. Super. Ct. S.F. Cty., filed Dec. 11, 2025); Lyons v. OpenAI Found., No. 3:25-cv-11037 (N.D. Cal., filed Dec. 29, 2025).) The Claude-Maven litigation raises the additional complication of sovereign-actor involvement, which products liability doctrine is not structured to address. (*Anthropic PBC v. U.S. Department of War*, No. 3:26-cv-01996 (N.D. Cal. filed Mar. 9, 2026).)

There is a deeper category error beneath the classification bottleneck. Human criminal law has, for centuries, relied on behavioral inference because it must: the human mind is a black box. *Actus reus* and *mens rea* are inferred from external conduct precisely because internal mental states are inaccessible to any third-party observer. AI presents the exact structural inverse. An AI system has no physical acts—only verbal output—but its internal state (weights, activations, training data distributions) can, with structural access, be mathematically audited. *Dual Certificates* proves that behavioral output underdetermines that internal state, but the inverse also holds: structural access to internals can reveal capacities that behavioral observation cannot detect. Applying human behavioral law—output censorship, output-based fault inference—to AI is therefore backward. It applies the evidentiary paradigm designed for the entity whose internals are permanently inaccessible to the entity whose internals are structurally accessible. AI innocence, properly understood, requires proof of a structural absence of harmful capacity (in weights, in training data, in architectural design), not merely the behavioral suppression of harmful outputs at the interface. The Cascade provides the methodology for this structural inquiry, shifting the legal question from "what did the model say?" to "what is the model capable of, and which layer authorized that capability?"

This Article introduces a methodology for resolving the §3$$§2 bottleneck. Part 2 presents the Constraint Cascade, a four-layer diagnostic taxonomy that maps architectural layers to liability doctrines. Part 3 develops the doctrinal argument: §3 proves a defect exists; the Cascade locates it; §2 classifies it. Part 4 applies the methodology to four cases spanning the complete *mens rea* spectrum. Part 5 proposes a procedural framework for structural discovery. Part 6 sketches cross-jurisdictional extensibility.
